# Caught between export dynamism and lagging consumption, Argentina navigates an uneven recovery

The Argentine economy enters the second half of 2026 moving at two speeds: a primary and energy sector posting record figures, and a cluster of activities tied to the domestic market that has yet to gain traction, against a backdrop in which exchange-rate stability has consolidated but credit is beginning to show signs of strain.

The official exchange rate closed the last business day at $1,460 on the sell side at Banco Nación's boards, while the blue-chip dollar traded at $1,435 — an unprecedented convergence that reflects the impact of the capital-controls exit implemented in April. Financial dollars hovered in similar territory: the MEP at $1,456.76 and the contado con liquidación at $1,471.45. The narrowing of the gap between the various FX segments is one of the most visible achievements of Javier Milei's framework, but it is not enough to mask the fragility of the activity picture.

Economist Gabriel Caamaño, head of consultancy Outlier, summed up that dilemma with surgical precision in an interview with La Nación: "The economy is not in recession, but there is also nothing that allows me to say it's flying." His diagnosis frames activity as the government's hardest target within a triad of variables — reserve accumulation, disinflation, and reactivation — that rarely move in the same direction. Inflation has receded and reserves are accumulating with more conviction than in 2025, but growth came in weak in the first quarter and April data did not improve the picture. For Caamaño, the structural weakness of the scheme lies in the absence of a defined and credible monetary regime capable of efficiently anchoring expectations.

Official March data confirm that reading: excluding the primary sector, activity remained at a level barely below that of February 2025. Agriculture and energy, by contrast, grew 12.8% over the same period, driven by a record harvest and expanding oil output. It is in that gap where much of the interpretive dispute between the government and private analysts resides, according to La Nación. Even so, some signs point to a possible trend break toward an expansionary cycle, albeit a narrow and uncertain one. The question with the greatest electoral weight is whether sectors such as construction, commerce, and industry — the largest job creators — will begin to join the recovery.

The flip side of that question comes from the province of Buenos Aires, where household bank delinquency has nearly quadrupled in a year and already affects one in six adults, according to data compiled by Ámbito Financiero. Banco Provincia president Juan Cuattromo warned that credit has come to replace lost income, and that without real wage recovery there is no sustainable exit from over-indebtedness. A bill is already circulating in the provincial legislature to protect consumers in situations of financial vulnerability. The signal is unmistakable: the credit expansion the government celebrates as a vote of confidence has a less luminous face on family balance sheets.

While the domestic market processes those tensions, the export front continues to advance with firm steps. YPF's board approved this week its incorporation as a supplier to Transportadora de Gas del Sur's NGL project, an initiative of roughly $3 billion aimed at producing and exporting propane, butane, and natural gasoline from Bahía Blanca. TGS CEO Oscar Sardi confirmed to La Nación that the company is hours away from formally filing for the Large Investment Incentive Regime (RIGI). The competitive edge is concrete: while standard natural gas contains 10% liquefiable components, Vaca Muerta's runs between 25% and 30%, turning the project into a high-value-added bet on the geological wealth of the Neuquén basin. Neuquén, in fact, leads the investment-dynamism ranking among Argentine provinces, according to a study cited by Ámbito Financiero.

That export strength, however, runs into a structural obstacle that the record harvest and the energy boom cannot resolve on their own: logistics. Raúl Robin, director of Regional Economies at CAME, told La Nación that Argentine logistics is the most expensive in South America, roughly 24% above Brazil's and as much as 40% higher than Chile's on some components. The most illustrative figure is repeated by producers and specialists alike: shipping a container from China to Buenos Aires costs less than moving a load from a town in Salta to the port. That distortion quietly erodes the export competitiveness that the exchange rate and the RIGI aim to build.

On the financial front, Argentine investors remain focused on the SpaceX IPO, scheduled for June 12 on the New York Stock Exchange. The deal aims to raise $75 billion at a $1.77 trillion valuation, which would make it the largest initial public offering in history. The research team at IOL Inversiones described it as "a bet as ambitious as it is risky," pointing to a 15% slowdown in first-quarter revenue and operating losses of $1.9 billion attributable to the development of xAI.

In the coming weeks, attention will turn to April and May activity data, which will determine whether the green shoots in industry and construction take hold or fade. The trajectory of real wages and bank delinquency will set the pulse of consumption; the formal RIGI filing of the NGL project, that of private investment. The government needs both fronts to advance simultaneously — and soon, with the electoral calendar already breathing down its neck.
